Analytics for policy and scientific inference are activities that use the same methods to analyze data, but that weigh the evidence and penalize uncertainty in very different ways. This document describes a bespoke analytics framework for malaria policy.

We start with the expectation that the data we are most likely to have is malaria prevalence data. Our goal is to reconstruct a coherent picture of malaria transmission that ends up estimating the local emergence rates of mosquitoes. This is, in some ways, bass ackwards from what most people think about malaria, in which mosquito ecology is the exogenous forcing factor. We agree that mosquito ecology is the primary exogenous forcing factor, and that we will usually be able to make reasonably good guesses about malaria using some combination of data describing topography, habitat, and weather. History has demonstrated quite clearly that when analysis of environmental variables are untethered from malaria data, the results are usually nonsense. Our view is that we need to know about mosquito ecology, but that we will lack data describing mosquito populations, and that malaria transmission has usually been modified by malaria control. Under these assumptions, this analytics framework is designed to work with the data we have.

PR-Based Estimation

To make a good estimate, we will also need data describing:

  • Care Seeking
  • Travel & Human Mobility
  • Mosquito Mobility
  • Mosquito Bionomics
  • Vector Control

The algorithm works like this:

  1. Estimate total exposure

    • Given an estimate of the PfPR, what is the force of infection or \(\tau\)-day attack rate? Let \(h\) denote the FoI, and \(\alpha(\tau) = 1- e^{-h \tau}\) the \(\tau\)-day attack rate.

    • The relationship between exposure and prevalence is modified by care seeking, so we will need to know what fraction of incident infections are treated and cured. Typically, this relationship will vary by age.

  2. Estimate local exposure

    • We regard estimates of total exposure as being a combination of three things: exposure at home, exposure near home, and exposure while travelling.

    • To estimate exposure at and around home, we need to formulate models for the time at risk, denoted \(\Psi\)

    • To estimate exposure while traveling, we will need some information about travel infection, \(\delta\).

  3. Convert estimates of FoI / AR into an estimate of the EIR.

  4. Convert estimates of the EIR into an estimate of the HBR. In the basic formula, the EIR is the product of the HBR and the SR. In this case, it is based on some version of Macdonalds mosquito model. We need:

    • mosquito bionomic parameters (\(f\), \(Q\), \(g\))
    • the parasites extrinsic incubation period (EIP or \(n\))

  5. Convert estimates of the HBR into an estimate of the emergence rate of adult mosquitoes. We need estimates of:

    • human population density (\(H\))

    • mosquito movement (\(\Omega\))

Elaboration

A key step in robust analytics is to recognize that at each step, we must make an assumption about something we dont know for certain. We can characterize total uncertainty about the past by considering two main sources:

  • Mechanistic uncertainty or uncertainty about how malaria works. This applies to models describing malaria.

  • Uncertainty about the context. This applies to models describing malaria here.

We would like our knowledge of a place to improve over time. We would also like to avoid the problem of excessive computation to get where we want to be, so we start with simple models.
