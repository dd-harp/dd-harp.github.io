Intro

.

.

It is not a simple thing to define the word model. In general, a model represents or imitates something else. In some cases, that something else is a plan or an ideal.

There is always a devil hiding in the details, and nuance matters. One generalized attempt to define model is the Merriam-Webster dictionary, which defines model in 13 ways as a noun, in five as a transitive verb, in two as an intransitive verb, and in two more as an adjective. In the work I do every day, the word model could be used to describe algorithms operating at multiple levels of abstraction: a concept; a simple functional form; a statistical model; a mechanistic model; a family of models; a part of a model; the mathematical description; or the code that implements it. In this essay, I discuss the use of models in scientific research and in analysis done with the intention of formulating policy advice.

I want to dispense with a common misperception (or facile criticism) right up front. Over the years, Ive heard some very naive critiques about models and policy. Many have argued that we should be using data to guide our decisions and not models. I call this naive because it ignores the simple fact that a model will always sit between the data and the decision. The real question is what are the best sorts of models to use.

To see what I mean, consider the fact that none of us are omniscient. What we know is filtered through observations. We experience the world through sensory inputs, and our brains make sense of what we see and hear. Our minds look for patterns. To make sense of these observations, we develop models or representations of these patterns, and we turn them into stories. Cognition, concepts, measurement, and analysis of all sorts are all based on a model of some sort. Models and representation are at the most basic level the way we think. The problem goes beyond the sciences. Since we are not omniscient, and so we must grapple with this two step process: observation and representation. What we perceive and what is real. We are always grappling to find a new knowledge of reality as Wallace Stevens put it in his poem, Not ideas about the thing but the thing itself.

I have always loved a poem by Rilke, A God Can Do It, that grapples with this concept in its own way. I never know what other people take from poetry. For me the poem evokes many of my own experiences when I have felt moved in some way by music, by a story, or by a painting.

Anyone who pretends all this is straightforward and simple is a fool.

Rilke

Simulacra

In the arts and philosophy, the study of representation is called mimetics.

Some basic ideas in mimetics have found their way into popular culture. In the movie The Matrix, Neo wakes up from a dream and his computer starts texting him spontaneously. It advises him to follow the white rabbit. There is a knock at the door. Someone wants something from him. Neo pulls out a book. The guts of the book have been stripped out, and the cover merely holds some disks with code. A woman has a white rabbit tatoo, and Neo follows it. Like Alice, wants to see how deep the rabbit hole goes. Eventually, he discovers that the world he has known is not real. Neo has been living in two worlds. One of them is a computer simulation.

The book that Neo is holding in that scene is Simulacrum and Simulation, by Jean Baudrillard.

The notion of a simulacrum is part of a very serious critique of culture.

While the notion of simulation should be familiar to scientists, the notion of a simulacrum may not be. The word simulacrum was first used in the late 16th century to describe a representation of a thing a painting or sculpture especially of a god. Over time, it came to mean a mimetic work that was further and further removed from reality. A fun exercise is to do a google image search for hyperrealism or simulacrum.

To Baudrillard, the simulacrum is something that comes to stand in for that which is real.

The simulacrum is never that which conceals the truthit is the truth which conceals that there is none. The simulacrum is true.

To see what Baudrillard is trying to say, we must delve into the way cultures come to deal with reality and meaning through signs and symbols. Baudrillard argues that society was saturated with simulacra. The process of adopting these simulacra happens in four steps.

The following was copied and pasted from the wikipedea page.

  1. The first stage is a faithful image/copy, where we believe, and it may even be correct, that a sign is a reflection of a profound reality (pg 6), this is a good appearance, in what Baudrillard called the sacramental order.
  2. The second stage is perversion of reality, this is where we come to believe the sign to be an unfaithful copy, which masks and denatures reality as an evil appearanceit is of the order of maleficence. Here, signs and images do not faithfully reveal reality to us, but can hint at the existence of an obscure reality which the sign itself is incapable of encapsulating.
  3. The third stage masks the absence of a profound reality, where the sign pretends to be a faithful copy, but it is a copy with no original. Signs and images claim to represent something real, but no representation is taking place and arbitrary images are merely suggested as things which they have no relationship to. Baudrillard calls this the order of sorcery, a regime of semantic algebra where all human meaning is conjured artificially to appear as a reference to the (increasingly) hermetic truth.
  4. The fourth stage is pure simulacrum, in which the simulacrum has no relationship to any reality whatsoever. Here, signs merely reflect other signs and any claim to reality on the part of images or signs is only of the order of other such claims. This is a regime of total equivalency, where cultural products need no longer even pretend to be real in a nave sense, because the experiences of consumers lives are so predominantly artificial that even claims to reality are expected to be phrased in artificial, hyperreal terms. Any nave pretension to reality as such is perceived as bereft of critical self-awareness, and thus as oversentimental.

Some of these ideas are also relevant for the sciences, or more particularly, for scientific culture. Think about the meaning of words like scientific theory. Baudrillards ideas offers a very serious criticism of the way this two-step process gets translated in cultures, and the myriad ways it can get derailed. In fact, Baudrillard would argue that it almost inevitably gets derailed.

If this simulacrum concept is not resonating at the moment, think about the difference between the conversations that take place about the same thing at different institutions: the team that sprays houses, the scientist studying the effects of IRS, and the bureaucrats in various institutions.

I have introduced the notion of a simulacrum because it is, potentially, a very serious criticism of the use of models. Anyone who thinks that it is a criticism of mechanistic models is missing the point. It also applies to informal models the sorts of models in an an experts head: the data go in and advice comes out, but what happens in between is never written down. Arguably, the process of writing down models so they are open to scrutiny, making specific and verifiable predictions, and constantly challenging the models with the data achieves Baudrillards sacramental order.

Context

The question What makes a model good? depends, to a large extent, on why the model was developed.

There can be dozens of reasons for developing models. One reason for building a model, for example, is to teach people basic principles about how malaria transmission works. For this, a simple Ross-Macdonald model is ideal.

A question that has shaped my thinking a lot over the past few years deals with the slightly modified question, What sorts of models are good for policy? In particular, what sorts of models should we develop if we want to stratify populations and landscapes and give advice that is tailored to context. How do we use the data we have to evaluate policies? How to we make recommendations about the optimal allocation of resources to various intervention combinations? How do we evaluate the uncertainty when data are missing? Thinking about these sorts of questions, I started asking about the difference between building models of malaria and building models of malaria transmission in Zanzibar, or any specific location. If we are to give advice that is tailored to context, we must be able to identify the features of a landscape that could lead us to make very different conclusions. How well do existing models serve those needs?

On the one hand, after sitting in dozens of meetings over the years, the advice coming from models has actually seemed quite generic.

The first thing to do, lets kill the mosquitoes.

Generically, this is very good advice. An equally good piece of generically very good advice:

We should prioritize treating every case of severe malaria.

Advice, it would seem, follows common sense.

On the other hand, the outcomes of malaria control have been highly heterogeneous. Doing the same thing in different places has produced highly heterogeneous outcomes. In some places, the vectors populations have gone locally extinct. In other places, intensive vector control has scarcely made a dent in transmission. What accounts for the differences?

If were just building models of malaria, and not models of malaria in a place, then they are complex metaphors. Informally, we can use the models to trace out advice, and we get a kind of syllogism. We identify a priori the features that could potentially affect malaria, and then we follow a kind of logic:

  1. In places that had features {A, B, C}, the optimal set of interventions would be {x,y,z}.
  2. This place has features {A,B,C}.
  3. Therefore, we recommend {x,y,z}

The logic is unassailable, if we accept the premise. How do we know if we should trust all the steps that led to this syllogism? Important questions are how a model was developed to trace out a generic set of features, and how sure we are that this place has those features. More to the point, the larger problem comes when a program comes back and says:

Ive done {x,y,z}, as recommended, but I still have malaria. What should I do next?

It might be possible to trace out the answers a priori but such approaches seem to lead into The Garden of Forking Paths.

The RAMP-Model-Library was an attempt to take a different approach to the same problem. Rather than try to identify a priori what features would affect malaria, we tried to develop a way of modeling malaria transmission in situ. In doing so, we knew up front that we would have very difficult time calibrating the models, because the amount of data available to inform modeling decisions is always limited. On the other hand, we wanted models that could be tailored over time to fit what has been learned about transmission in context. This would require building models that were modular and that did not constrain the level of complexity. A framework was needed with scalable complexity.

Inference

.

Since every policy decision is based on some sort of model, the question is what sorts of models make the best use of evidence.

In science, we can escape becoming a simulacrum by constantly checking predictions against reality, against data. This makes it possible to stay in Baudrillards sacramental order.

At the same time, we must acknowledge that there are limitations on how well we can measure those factors that will

How do we know that our model remains an accurate representation of the thing? The answer for us is that we must always try and link it to the evidence. Science is ultimately a cultural process, but the process of translating evidence and policy can avoid becoming a simulacrum if we are constantly checking the accuracy of the predictions.

RAMP is an attempt to build in situ models.

The only question is what sort of model works best.

In science, we turn observations into data, and we make sense of that data through models. We graph the data and look for patterns. We use statistical methods to describe the data.

Policy

The best models address the available data and the needs of a study. These two phrases can pull a study in very different directions, and it is not always possible to know what the best compromise might be.
